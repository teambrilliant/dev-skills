# The Six-Page Narrative

Amazon replaced slide decks with written prose memos for proposals, business
reviews, and operating plans. Use this for any significant decision document that
isn't a product launch (for launches, use the PR-FAQ).

## The format

- **Prose, not bullets.** Full sentences and paragraphs. Bullet-only slides let
  weak logic hide in the gaps; prose forces the argument to be complete.
- **Six pages max.** Scale the limit to the meeting: ~1 page per 10 minutes of
  reading time. The limit is a forcing function — it makes you prioritize.
- **Self-contained.** A reader who wasn't in prior meetings should follow it.
- **Optional sections:**
  - **Tenets** — the foundational assumptions the proposal rests on, stated so
    readers can challenge them directly.
  - **FAQ** — preempt the likely objections (a pre-mortem on your own argument).
  - **Appendices** — supporting data and detail; not required in-session reading.

## How the meeting runs

1. Distribute the narrative **at the start of the meeting** (not before).
2. Everyone **reads silently** — about 20 minutes for a six-pager. No presenter,
   no commentary during the read.
3. When all signal they're done, discussion opens. The author does **not**
   re-walk the document verbally — the writing already made the case.
4. A **dedicated note-taker** (not the author) captures the discussion.

## Why it works

Writing a narrative is harder than making slides — and that's the point. The
effort of composing a clear, honest argument in prose surfaces the holes the
author would otherwise gloss over. The silent read gives every idea a fair
hearing on its merits, not on the presenter's delivery. Reading together also
means the room shares the same context before anyone reacts.

**Heuristic:** if you're reaching for a slide deck to make a real decision,
write the memo instead. If the memo is hard to write, the thinking isn't done.
