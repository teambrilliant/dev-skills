# System Traps — archetypes of recurring failure

Donella Meadows' catalog of **system archetypes**: structures that reliably
produce bad behavior no matter who's involved. Load this when a system *resists
fixes*, *drifts*, or *keeps reproducing a problem* — match the situation to an
archetype, then apply its structural escape. The pattern is the diagnosis; the
escape is almost always "change the structure," not "try harder."

## The 8 traps

### Policy Resistance
- **Structure:** multiple actors pull a shared stock toward conflicting goals; every effective fix triggers countermoves that cancel it, so the system stays where nobody wants it.
- **Escape:** stop fighting for the narrow goal — bring the actors together and redirect their combined energy toward a shared overarching goal.

### Tragedy of the Commons
- **Structure:** a shared, erodable resource with no feedback from its condition back to individual users; each rationally over-uses while costs spread to all → ruin.
- **Escape:** restore the missing feedback — educate on collective cost, privatize so users bear their own cost, or regulate access by agreement.

### Drift to Low Performance
- **Structure:** perceived performance is biased toward bad news; goals slide to match slipping results → a reinforcing downward spiral (boiled frog).
- **Escape:** hold standards absolute — better, anchor the goal to *best* past performance, turning the same loop into an upward drift.

### Escalation
- **Structure:** each actor sets its goal relative to a rival and tries to stay ahead → reinforcing one-upmanship (arms race, price war) that grows until collapse.
- **Escape:** refuse to compete (unilaterally break the loop), or negotiate a structural bound — limits, treaties, regulation — that adds balancing feedback.

### Success to the Successful
- **Structure:** winners are rewarded with the resources to win again → reinforcing concentration until one takes all (competitive exclusion).
- **Escape:** level periodically — diversify into new niches, cap the winner's share (antitrust), or redistribute to restore others' ability to compete.

### Shifting the Burden to the Intervenor (Addiction)
- **Structure:** a symptomatic fix relieves pain without addressing the cause and *erodes the system's own capacity* → reinforcing dependency.
- **Escape:** never use symptom-only fixes on a capable system; if already dependent, rebuild the system's own capacity *before* withdrawing the crutch.

### Rule Beating
- **Structure:** rigid or poorly-designed rules trigger creative evasion — actors satisfy the letter while defeating the purpose, producing perverse distortions.
- **Escape:** read rule-beating as feedback on a bad rule — redesign it so the system's creativity serves the rule's actual intent, not an end-run around it.

### Seeking the Wrong Goal
- **Structure:** a system delivers exactly what its loops are measured and optimized for; if the indicator is a poor proxy for real welfare, you get the proxy and miss the point.
- **Escape:** define indicators that reflect the system's *real* purpose; never confuse effort or throughput with actual result.

## How to use it

1. Describe the misbehavior (resists fixes? drifts down? one party wins
   everything? a fix made it worse?).
2. Match to the archetype whose *structure* fits — not whose symptom sounds
   similar.
3. Apply the structural escape. If the proposed fix doesn't change the
   structure, expect the trap to reassert itself.

**One-line verdict pattern:** "This is *Shifting the Burden* — the fix is making
the team less able to solve it themselves. Rebuild capacity before adding more
support."
