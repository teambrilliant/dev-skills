# Leverage Points — where to intervene in a system

Donella Meadows' ranked list of 12 places to intervene, **least → most
powerful**. Load this when a "where do we intervene?" / "what will actually move
this?" question comes up. The whole point is counterintuitive: almost everyone
pushes the *bottom* of this list (and often in the wrong direction); durable
change lives near the *top*.

## The ranked 12 (12 = weakest, 1 = strongest)

| # | Leverage point | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| 12 | **Numbers** — constants & parameters | Subsidies, taxes, standards, budgets, staffing levels. Where ~99% of attention goes; rarely changes behavior. Adjusting a dial. |
| 11 | **Buffers** — stabilizing stocks vs. flows | A big stock relative to its flows is more stable. Increasing buffer capacity stabilizes, but buffers are usually physical and slow to change. |
| 10 | **Stock-and-flow structure** — physical plumbing | The layout of nodes and pathways. Huge effect, but rebuilding physical structure is the slowest/costliest move — leverage is getting the design right up front. |
| 9 | **Delays** — lengths relative to rate of change | Feedback delays cause oscillation and overshoot. Changing a delay can swing behavior — but delays are often fixed; usually easier to *slow the system* than shorten the delay. |
| 8 | **Balancing feedback loops** — strength | The self-correcting loops that hold a stock near a goal. Strengthen/restore them (monitoring, accountability, real signals) to add resilience. |
| 7 | **Reinforcing feedback loops** — gain | Self-multiplying growth/erosion. *Slowing* a reinforcing loop is usually more powerful than strengthening a balancing one — and far better than letting it run. |
| 6 | **Information flows** — who knows what | Add or restore *missing* feedback — deliver information to a place it wasn't reaching. Not tuning an existing loop; creating a new one. Cheap and powerful. |
| 5 | **Rules** — incentives, punishments, constraints | Rules define scope, boundaries, freedom. Whoever controls the rules holds real power; rules govern behavior far more deeply than any number. |
| 4 | **Self-organization** — power to change structure | The system's ability to add/change/evolve its own loops, rules, and structure. The strongest form of resilience; requires diversity and room to experiment. |
| 3 | **Goals** — the purpose of the system | What the whole system is *for*. Even self-organizing behavior conforms to it. Change the goal and everything below reorients. |
| 2 | **Paradigm** — the mindset the system arises from | The shared, unstated assumptions from which goals, rules, and structure emerge. Paradigm shifts transform systems utterly. |
| 1 | **Power to transcend paradigms** | Staying unattached to *any* paradigm — recognizing no worldview is the truth. The basis for radical flexibility and the deepest, longest-lasting leverage. |

## How to use it

1. Locate where the proposed intervention sits on the list.
2. If it's at 12–9 (numbers, buffers, structure, delays), ask: is there a
   higher-leverage move at 6–3 (information flow, a rule, the goal)?
3. Check direction — people often push the right point the *wrong* way
   (e.g. adding a parameter that strengthens a runaway reinforcing loop).
4. Name the highest-leverage point that's actually *reachable* given constraints,
   not just the theoretically strongest.

**One-line verdict pattern:** "This is a #12 move (tweak the number). The real
leverage is #5 — the rule that lets the behavior happen. Change that instead."
